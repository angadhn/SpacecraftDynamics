Prepared by: Noah Leigh, Ilanthiraiyan Sivaganamoorthy, and Angadh Nanjangud

So far, we have studied some general aspects of orbital dynamics without focusing on any specifics shapes of orbits. Here, our main goal is to address this by diving deeper into elliptic orbits. The following sections will support us in learning about elliptic orbits:

  2. The Equation of the Orbit

The Eccentricity Vector#

Deriving The Eccentricity Vector#

The Eccentricity vector \({\bf e}\) helps us describe the shape of an orbit and its direction. We can find it by taking the cross product of Equation (31) with the specific angular momentum \(\bf h\):

(74)#\[\ddot{{\bf r}}\times{\bf h} = - \frac{\mu}{r^3} {\bf r}\times{\bf h}\]

Using the product rule for differentiation, we see that

(75)#\[\begin{split}\frac{d}{dt}(\dot{{\bf r}} \times {\bf h}) &= \ddot{{\bf r}} \times {\bf h} + \dot{{\bf r}} \times \dot{{\bf h}} \\ &= \ddot{{\bf r}} \times {\bf h},\end{split}\]

where we note that \(\dot{\bf{h}} = 0\) because \(\bf{h}\) is constant, as shown previously. Therefore, we have shown that the left-hand side of Equation (74) can be written as:

\[\ddot{{\bf r}} \times {\bf h} = \frac{d}{dt}(\dot{{\bf r}} \times {\bf h})\]

Now, we focus on the RHS of Equation (74), which can be written as a vector triple product and then massaged to yield:

Note

Using the rule for \({\bf a}\times ({\bf b}\times{{\bf c}} )=({\bf c}\cdot{\bf a}){\bf b}-({\bf b}\cdot{\bf a}){\bf c}\) with \({\bf h}={\bf r}\cdot\dot{{\bf r}}\) :

(76)#\[\begin{split}- \frac{\mu}{r^3} {\bf r}\times{\bf h} &= - \frac{\mu}{r^3} {\bf r} \times\Bigl( {\bf r} \times \dot{\bf r}\Bigr)\\ &=-\frac{\mu}{r^3} \Bigl[ (\dot{{\bf r}}\cdot{\bf r}){\bf r}-({\bf r}\cdot{\bf r})\dot{{\bf r}} \Bigr]\\ &=-\frac{\mu}{r^3}(\dot{r}r{\bf r}-r^2\dot{{\bf r}})\\ &=-\frac{\mu}{r^2}(\dot{r}{\bf r}-r\dot{{\bf r}})\end{split}\]

Now, take a moment to consider the time derivative of the RHS of Equation (74) (and remembering that \(\mu\) is constant) gives the same result as Equation (76)

(77)#\[\frac{d}{dt} (\mu \frac{\bf r}{r} ) = - \frac{\mu}{r^2}(\dot{r}{\bf r} - r\dot{\bf r})\]

thus showing that the RHS of Equation (74) is the same as the LHS of Equation (76). That is:

\[\frac{d}{dt}(\dot{{\bf r}} \times {\bf h}) = \frac{d}{dt} (\mu \frac{\bf r}{r} )\]

or that

(78)#\[\dot{{\bf r}}\times{\bf h} - \mu\frac{{\bf r}}{r} = constant.\]

We call this entire vectors that results on the LHS of Equation (78) as the eccentricty vector \(\bf e\), which can also be written as:

Note

We have divided throughout Equation (78) by \(\mu\), as this doesn’t really affect things on either side of the equation.

(79)#\[{\bf e}=\frac{\dot{{\bf r}}\times{\bf h}}{\mu}-\frac{{\bf r}}{r} \]

Properties of \(\bf e\)#

Note

Can you explain why \({\bf e}\) lies in the plane of motion?

  1. Again, we remind you that we have derived that \({\bf e}\) is a constant vector. This is implying it is both constant in direction and magnitude. Further, \({\bf e}\) lies in the plane of motion and is fixed. Thus we can arbitrarily choose a direction \(\hat{i}\) t be aligned with \({\bf e}\).

  2. Further, \(e = |{\bf e}| \geq 0\), by definition.

The Equation of the Orbit#

We derive the equation to the orbit using the eccentricity vector. We begin by taking the dot product of \({\bf e}\) with \({\bf r}\).

Note

recall \({\bf a}\cdot({\bf b}\times{\bf c})=({\bf a}\times{\bf b})\cdot{\bf c}\)

(80)#\[\begin{split}{\bf r}\cdot{\bf e} &= \frac{{\bf r}\cdot(\dot{{\bf r}}\times\dot{{\bf h}})}{\mu}-\frac{{\bf r}\cdot{\bf r}}{r}\\ &=\frac{({\bf r}\times\dot{{\bf r}})\cdot{\bf h}}{\mu} - \frac{r^2}{r}\\ &= \frac{h^2}{\mu}-r\end{split}\]

But we also know that \({\bf r}\cdot{\bf e}= r e \cos(\theta)\), where \(\theta\) is the angle between \({\bf r}\) and \({\bf e}\) and is called the true anomaly. Then, we can rewrite Equation (80) as

Note

In some textbooks, \(\theta\) is written by other symbols such as \(\nu\) or \(f\).

\[r e \cos(\theta) = \frac{h^2}{\mu}-r\]

or we can solve for \(r\) as

(81)#\[r = \frac{\frac{h^2}{\mu}}{1+ e \cos\left(\theta\right)}\]

This shows that \(r\) is the equation to an ellipse and, in fact, yields Kepler’s 1st Law which states that the orbit of a planet is an ellipse with the sun at one of the two focii.

Actually, \(r\) describes a different conic depending on the eccentricity (a constant that is determined by initial conditions). The eccentricity, \(e=|{\bf e}| \geq 0\), determines the shape of the orbit. Substituting for the different values of \(e\), as given below, into Equation (81) can yield different orbits:

  • Circular orbit: \(e = 0 \Rightarrow r = h^2/\mu = const\)

  • Elliptic orbit: \(0 < e < 1 \Rightarrow r_{min} < r < r_{max} \)

  • Parabolic orbit: \(e = 1 \Rightarrow \frac{1}{2}h^2/\mu \leq r \leq \infty\)

  • Hyperbolic orbit: \(e > 1 \Rightarrow \frac{h^2/\mu}{1+e} \leq r \leq \infty \)

Tip

  • Operative orbits are always circular or elliptic so as to keep a satellite close to the planet of interest for as long as possible.

  • Hyperbolic orbits are essentially maneuvers for interplanetary missions, typically to reduce propellant use by using the gravity of a body to reach another target body (also known as gravity assist maneuver).

Key Parameters of Elliptic Orbits#

Aside from the equations shown within Fig. 9, we also can determine three other parameters:

  1. Semi-latus rectum(\(p\)): This is the distance from the foci (orbitted body) to the orbit measured perpendicular to the semi-major axis. It can be computed from Equation (81) by setting \(\theta = 90^\circ\)

    (82)#\[r = \frac{h^2}{\mu} = p\]

    More generally, we can then rewrite Equation (81) using \(p\) as

    (83)#\[r = \frac{p}{1 + e \cos\theta} = \frac{a(1 - e^2)}{1 + e \cos\theta}\]

    where,

    (84)#\[p = a(1 - e^2)\]

Note

If a satellite is orbiting the moon (mass \(m_2\) in Fig. 9), then we specifically use terms perilune and apolune instead of periapsis and apoapsis. If \(m_2\) is the Earth, then we use the terms perigee and apogee.

  1. Periapsis is the value of \(r\) at \(\theta = 0^\circ\) and is denoted \(r_p\). It can be obtained from Equation (83) as

    (85)#\[r_p = \frac{p}{1+e}= a(1 - e)\]

  2. Apoapsis is the value of \(r\) at \(\theta = 180^\circ\) and is denoted \(r_a\). . It can be obtained from Equation (83) as

    (86)#\[r_a = a(1 + e)\]

Determination of the Orbit#

Typically you will be given \({\bf r_o}\) and \({\bf v_o}\) and will be asked to compute \({\bf h}\) and \({\bf e}\). The process for doing so is:

  1. Compute the semi-latus rectum \(p\) from Equation (82).

  2. Compute the semi-major axis, \(a\), using \(a=\frac{p}{1-e^2}\) (shown in Fig. 9).

  3. Compute the apsides points (most extreme points in the orbit) using (85) and (86) .

Vis-Viva Equation#

The Vis-Viva Equation is one of the most useful equations in space mission design as it is used to find the velocity at any given \(r\) when a maneuver is planned.

The total energy of the orbit is the sum of the kinetic energy and the potential energies of the orbit. The sum of the potential energies is the effective potential energy, \(\phi_{eff}\):

Tip

Recall that \({\bf v} = \dot{r}\hat{\bf e}_r + r\dot{\theta}\hat{\bf e}_\theta\). In terms of \(h\), this becomes \({\bf v} = \dot{r}\hat{\bf e}_r + \frac{h}{r^2}\hat{\bf e}_\theta\). From this, we can now easily compute the magnitude of the velocity as seen in Equation (87).

(87)#\[\begin{split}E &= \frac{1}{2} v^2-\frac{\mu}{r}\\ &= \frac{1}{2} \dot{r^2} + \frac{1}{2} \frac{h^2}{r^2} - \frac{\mu}{r}\\ &= \frac{1}{2} \dot{r^2} + \phi_{eff}\end{split}\]

At perigee, \(r_p\), and apogee, \(r_a\), the velocity term (in Equation (87)) \(\dot r = 0\) so \(E=\phi_{eff}\). Thus, we can derive an equation for the specific orbital energy as a function of \(a\).

Tip

We make use of \(r_p\) and \(p\) defined in Equations (86) and (84), respectively.

(88)#\[\begin{split}E({r_p})&=\phi_{eff}({r_p})\\ &= \frac{1}{2} \frac{h^2}{r_p^2} - \frac{\mu}{r_p}\\ &= \frac{1}{2} \frac{\mu p}{r_p^2} - \frac{\mu}{r_p}\\ &= -\frac{\mu(1-e)}{2a (1-e)}\\ E&=-\frac{\mu}{2a}\end{split}\]

The specific orbital energy is thus solely a function of \(a\). Orbits with the same semi-major axis have the same specific orbital energy.

The Vis-Viva Equation is obtained by equating Equations (86) and (88) as

Vis Viva Equation

(89)#\[\frac{1}{2}v^2 - \frac{\mu}{r}= -\frac{\mu}{2a}\]

Conclusion#

  • The eccentricity vector is a constant vector in orbital mechanics.

  • The orbit equation describes different conic sections based on the magnitude of the eccentricity vector.

  • The Vis Viva equation (89) is a crucial tool in mission planning for calculating velocities at various points in an orbit.
